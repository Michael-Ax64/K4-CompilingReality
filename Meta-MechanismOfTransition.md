## I. The Exercise

Place yourself at the centroid of a tetrahedron — the natural origin, the interior point equidistant from all four vertices, the integrating center from which all four faces are held. Not a vertex promoted to reference; the intrinsic center that requires no arbitrary choice. (Cartesian coordinates exile the origin to a corner and lose a dimension in the process. The tetrahedron has its origin already, at the centroid, holding the volume.)

Pick a destination outside the tetrahedron. Set a heading toward it. Engage — begin to move.

You carry three readouts. Each corresponds to one of the faces you are moving relative to, and each shows the tangent of the angle of your position relative to that face's plane.

Watch the readouts as you move.

Beginning at the centroid, moving toward a face: the tangent of your angle to that face's plane starts small and negative. You are inside; the face is ahead of you; the angle measured against the face is on the interior side, and its tangent is a small negative number.

As you approach the face, the angle opens. The tangent grows in magnitude — more negative, then steeply more negative.

At the instant you reach the face — the plane itself — the tangent passes through infinity. The angle is 90°; tan(90°) is undefined; the readout diverges. Approaching the plane from the inside, it runs to negative infinity. Crossing to the outside, it reappears at positive infinity.

You exit. The readout is now positive, decreasing in magnitude as you move into exterior space. The face is behind you. You are outside the tetrahedron you began inside.

That is the mechanism. Every word that follows unpacks what just happened.

## II. The Divergence Is the Boundary

The first thing the exercise establishes is that the crossing is not continuous in the interior coordinate.

The tangent readout does not pass smoothly from −0.9 to +0.9 as you cross the face. It runs to negative infinity, is *undefined at the plane itself*, and reappears at positive infinity. There is no value of the tangent at the boundary. The interior measure fails exactly there.

This failure is not a defect of the readout. It is the readout working correctly. The divergence of the tangent at the face is the signature that a genuine boundary has been reached — the surface past which the interior coordinate system cannot represent your position. If the readout passed smoothly through the face, the face would not be a boundary; it would be more interior, another region the same coordinates describe without interruption. The divergence is precisely what distinguishes a boundary from an interior gradient.

At the substrate scale, the physical instantiation of this Tangent Singularity is the Planck scale—the boundary width of the XOR bottleneck where classical coordinates fail.

This is the formal content of the Markov blanket. Inside and outside are conditionally independent given the blanket: the interior states do not connect to the exterior states except through the boundary, and the boundary is exactly where the interior's description stops being able to reach across. You cannot pass from inside to outside by extending the interior's coordinates, because the interior's coordinates diverge at the boundary. There is no smooth path across. The blanket is the surface at which the interior measure goes to infinity — which is to say, the surface at which the interior measure correctly reports that it has reached its own limit.

## III. The Two Infinities Are One Point

The second thing the exercise establishes is the reconciliation.

The tangent runs to negative infinity as you approach the plane from inside, and reappears at positive infinity as you continue into outside. These look like two events — a plunge to −∞ and a fall from +∞ — two infinities at the two ends of the crossing.

They are one point.

On the projective line, negative infinity and positive infinity are identified. They are not two distinct unbounded ends; they are a single point, the point at infinity, approached from two directions. The real number line, extended projectively, closes into a circle: travel far enough in the positive direction and you arrive where you would arrive traveling far enough in the negative direction. The two infinities meet. They are one.

So the passage through the face — the passage through the tangent's singularity — is the passage through the projective point at infinity. The −∞ you descend to as you approach the plane and the +∞ you descend from as you exit are the same point. The crossing is not a leap across a gap between two infinities. It is a passage through the single point where the two infinities are identified.

This is the reconciliation of the paradox. "Two infinities are one" is not a mystical assertion that has to be taken on faith. For the interior crossing, it is the literal projective structure of the boundary. The interior coordinate diverges to infinity at the face; the divergence on the two sides is the two ends; and the two ends are one point in the projective space the crossing occurs within. To pass through the boundary is to pass through the point where the interior measure's two infinities are revealed as a single point — and that revelation, that the descent into −∞ and the emergence from +∞ were never two, is the reconciliation the crossing delivers.

## IV. The Crossing Occurs Within a Space

The third thing the exercise establishes is the one that the keystone paper bounds, and it must be stated explicitly here, because it is the feature that makes this mechanism an account of *interior* crossings and not of the ultimate boundary.

Everything in the exercise occurs within a space.

The projective line on which the two infinities are identified is a space. The point at infinity is a point *in* that space — one element among others, available to be approached, reasoned about, passed through. The tetrahedron you began inside is embedded in a space. The exterior you cross into is a space, with coordinates that resume working on the far side: as you move into exterior space, the tangent readout becomes a finite positive number, decreasing in magnitude — the coordinates work again, you are in a new interior-of-something, oriented relative to new boundaries.

The crossing is a crossing *of something*, observed from a standpoint that holds both sides. There is an inside, an outside, and a boundary between — three things, all within a larger context that contains them all. The divergence of the interior coordinate at the boundary does not destroy the surrounding space; it marks one surface within it. On the far side, you have not left space. You have entered a new region of it, with new coordinates and new boundaries, themselves crossable by the same mechanism.

This is what makes the mechanism repeatable, and the crossings endless. Each crossing lands you in a new exterior that is itself an interior — a new space with new faces, each of which can be approached, each of which will diverge the interior measure at its plane, each of which can be passed through at its own point at infinity. Leaving the house, you enter the street, which has its own boundaries. Overcoming yourself, you enter a new self, with its own edges to overcome. The mechanism describes every one of these — every genuine transition between orders, every real reconciliation of two-infinities-into-one — and every one of them occurs within a space, lands in a space, and presents new boundaries to cross.

The mechanism describes interior crossings. It is true of them, exactly and formally. It does not describe the singularity — the ultimate boundary with no outside, not in the series, imaged by every crossing and identical to none. The point at infinity in this mechanism is a point in a space; the singularity is where space stops. To apply this mechanism to the ultimate boundary is to install an outside that is not there — to render the singularity as one more interior crossing, with one more surrounding space, one more far side reached. The mechanism carries this mark explicitly: it is the geometry of crossing *within* the world, true of every transition between orders, and bounded — by the keystone — from describing the boundary that has no within.
