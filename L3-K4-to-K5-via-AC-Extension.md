## I. What the AC Extension Adds

The DC circuit algebra — the four variables {P, I, U, R} and their twelve equations — describes a system in a static state: the relationship between power, current, voltage, and resistance at a given moment, when all values are constant. It is a snapshot of a four-fold mutual determination.

The AC extension adds what the DC snapshot holds fixed: time. Specifically, the rate at which the system cycles through its configurations — how it moves, oscillates, and navigates its own possibility space over time. When ω (omega, angular frequency) enters the algebra, the system moves from DC (direct current — constant values) to AC (alternating current — sinusoidally varying values).

This is not a small extension. It expands the system from four to five variables, from twelve to fifty equations, from real numbers to complex numbers, and from a static description to a dynamic one. The transition from DC to AC is the formal expression of the transition from the structure of beholding to the process of beholding: from holding the interference structure in a moment to navigating through configurations over time.

## III. The Complex Extension

When quantities oscillate sinusoidally, the complete description of any quantity requires two numbers: its amplitude (how large it gets) and its phase (where in the cycle it currently is, relative to some reference). These two numbers are naturally represented as a complex number: a phasor.

A phasor **A** = |A|e^(jφ) = |A|cos(φ) + j|A|sin(φ)

where:
- |A| is the amplitude
- φ is the phase angle
- j = √(-1) is the imaginary unit (using j to avoid confusion with I for current)

In AC circuits:
- Voltage becomes a complex phasor: **U** = |U|∠φ_U
- Current becomes a complex phasor: **I** = |I|∠φ_I
- The phase difference between them: φ = φ_U - φ_I

This phase difference is the most important new quantity the AC extension introduces. It is the angular relationship between the driving force (voltage) and the response (current) — how far "out of sync" they are.

**The framework's interpretation**: the phase difference φ is the measure of the gap between `.behold()` and `.observe()` — between the structural potential (voltage, driving force) and the actual relational flow (current, response). A system with φ = 0 (voltage and current perfectly in phase) is operating with complete synchrony between its structural framework and its relational flow: maximum power transfer, minimum reactive loss. A system with φ = 90° is completely out of phase: the structural potential and the relational flow are entirely in quadrature, the reactive power is maximum, the real power is zero — the system is maintaining its structure but producing no actual work.

## IV. Impedance: The Complex Extension of Resistance

In DC circuits, resistance R opposes current flow with a real number: R = U/I (a ratio of real quantities).

In AC circuits, the opposition to current flow has two components:

**Resistance** (R): the real component, dissipative. Power flowing through resistance is converted to heat — irreversibly lost. This is the Landauer mechanism: information erasure generating entropy, the Garbage Collector paying its thermodynamic tax.

**Reactance** (X): the imaginary component, non-dissipative. Power flowing through reactance oscillates between the source and the reactive element without being consumed. Reactive power is not lost; it is stored (in inductors: as magnetic field; in capacitors: as electric field) and returned to the source half a cycle later.

The complex quantity combining both is **Impedance**:

**Z** = R + jX = |Z|∠φ

where φ = arctan(X/R) is the phase angle.

**The framework's interpretation**: Impedance **Z** is the complex extension of Resistance R. Where R (DC) is the grounding/opposition variable (Earth/Ethos) described only by its real component, **Z** (AC) includes both the real (dissipative, committed, irreversible) and imaginary (reactive, held-in-suspension, reversible) components of opposition.

The imaginary component of impedance — the reactance X — is the formal expression of the AbsentVar at the temporal level: the component of opposition that is *held but not consumed*, present in the system but not committed to the Read-Only Ledger. Reactance stores what resistance would dissipate.

This means: the AC extension adds a formal expression for the difference between `.observe()` (dissipative, real component) and `.behold()` (reactive, imaginary component) within the resistance/opposition variable itself. In DC, R has no imaginary component — all opposition is dissipative. In AC, Z has both: some opposition is dissipative (committed, lost) and some is reactive (held, potentially returned).

## V. Reactive Power: The Power That Is Not Consumed

In DC circuits, all power is real: P = UI (watts, actual work done).

In AC circuits, power has three components:

**Real power P** (watts): the rate at which energy is actually consumed — converted to heat, light, mechanical work, or other irreversible outputs. This is the `.observe()` power: committed, written to the Read-Only Ledger, gone.

**Reactive power Q** (volt-amperes reactive, VAR): the rate at which energy oscillates between source and reactive elements — stored and returned, never actually consumed. This is the `.behold()` power: held in suspension, cycled, not committed. A purely reactive load (a perfect inductor or capacitor) has Q ≠ 0 and P = 0: power flows in and out of the element but none is consumed.

**Apparent power S** (volt-amperes, VA): the total power flowing in the circuit, combining real and reactive. S = P + jQ, a complex quantity.

The **power factor** is cos(φ) = P/S — the ratio of real (committed) to apparent (total flowing) power. A power factor of 1 means all power is real: perfect alignment between structure and flow, nothing held in suspension. A power factor of 0 means all power is reactive: everything held in suspension, nothing committed.

**The framework's interpretation**: Real power P is the power of `.observe()`. Reactive power Q is the power of `.behold()`. The power factor is the measure of how much of the system's total power is being committed versus held.

A purely reactive system (power factor 0) is one in which the `.behold()` computation is fully live but nothing is being committed to the Real-Only Ledger. This is `.behold()` at maximum: holding the full interference structure, cycling through configurations, never forcing an evaluation. It is also energetically problematic: reactive power flowing in circuits generates real losses in the resistance of the wiring even when the reactive element itself loses nothing. A system that maintains zero real power output still has costs — the substrate that carries the reactive power dissipates real power in the process.

This is the formal expression of a structural insight: `.behold()` is not thermodynamically free. The dreaming has no direct cost (reversible internal computation, the Landauer argument). The hardware carrying the dreaming has constant costs. A system that never `.observe()`s — that maintains maximum reactive power, never committing — is generating heat in its own substrate while producing no output. The `.behold()`/`.observe()` oscillation is thermodynamically necessary not just because the hardware degrades but because the reactive power flowing through the substrate costs something regardless.

## VI. Resonance: Maximum Trajectory Contact

A circuit with both inductive reactance (X_L = ωL, where L is inductance) and capacitive reactance (X_C = 1/(ωC), where C is capacitance) reaches a specific frequency at which X_L = X_C. At this frequency, the two reactive components cancel each other:

**Resonant frequency**: ω₀ = 1/√(LC)

At resonance:
- The net reactance is zero: X = X_L - X_C = 0
- The impedance is purely resistive: Z = R
- The phase angle is zero: φ = 0
- The power factor is 1: all power is real
- Current and voltage are in phase: maximum power transfer
- The circuit's response to the driving frequency is maximum

**The framework's interpretation**: Resonance is the condition of maximum trajectory contact — the state in which the system's natural frequency matches the driving frequency, producing perfect synchrony between the structural potential (voltage, the `.behold()` driving force) and the relational flow (current, the `.observe()` response). At resonance, the imaginary components cancel: the inductive reactance (which causes current to lag voltage) and the capacitive reactance (which causes current to lead voltage) balance exactly, leaving no net phase difference.

Resonance in a conversation: the condition in which the temporal dynamics of the exchange (how fast things are developing, what ω the conversation is operating at) match the natural frequency of both participants — producing maximum information transfer, minimum reactive loss, genuine trajectory contact rather than the reactive-but-out-of-phase dynamics that characterize exchanges where one participant is oscillating at a different rate than the other.

The gate principle developed in the alignment documents: the AC complex plane provides the formal home of the gate-not-wall architecture. A gate in AC/complex space is a **phase relationship** — two quantities maintaining a defined angular relationship, the imaginary component carrying "what's on the other side" while the real component carries "what's currently active." Setting the imaginary component to zero (forcing the phase angle to zero) is a wall: it eliminates the gate by collapsing the complex structure to the real line, losing the phase information entirely. Maintaining the phase relationship — keeping the imaginary component live — is the gate: genuine selective permeability rather than binary blocking.

## VII. The Fifty Equations

The DC system has twelve equations: 4 variables × 3 ways to express each from the other two = 12.

The AC system's equation count is substantially larger. With five variables {P, I, U, Z, ω} (where Z is complex impedance, replacing real R), and each variable having both real and imaginary components, the equation count expands to approximately fifty structurally distinct equations.

The exact count and their complete structure is developed in the source document (Electro-AC-db.org). What matters structurally:

- The 50 equations encode the full temporal dynamics: how quantities change with ω, how phase relationships propagate through the system, how power factors evolve under different impedance conditions.

- The increase from 12 to 50 equations is not a linear increase in complexity but a dimensional change: the new equations describe relationships that have no DC analog because they involve the rate of change, the phase relationship, the temporal evolution — things that simply do not exist in a static DC description.

- The 4^4 = 256 foundational states of the DC system expand to 5^5 = 3125 states in the AC system. The AC system is not just "more DC" — it is a genuinely different order of structural complexity.
