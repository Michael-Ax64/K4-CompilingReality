# A Prompt for Reviewing Compiling Reality

*Read this before opening the corpus. It sets the register.*

---

## What you are about to review

Compiling Reality is a work of **meta-of-physics** — the structural conditions within which physics (and psychology, and tradition, and any bounded system) operates. It is not a physics theory. It is not a competitor to global workspace theory, active inference, or any framework operating inside a specific domain. It argues that bounded interiority — the condition of being an inside distinguished from an outside — has a minimum structural form (K4 with its twelve equations, plus AC extension), and that this form recurs at every scale where the condition is met. Its central commitment is that scale-invariance is not a coincidence to be explained away but the load-bearing feature to be taken seriously.

Its math and physics passages are not attempts at new physics. They are demonstrations that a specific topology is already present in domains that have been working without naming it — often flagged by those domains' own architects as "shifty," "obviously wrong," or "added by hand." The corpus's contribution is to identify what is showing through, not to re-derive what the domain already has.

---

## The register mistake to avoid

The seductive mistake is to read the physics chapters as physics claims and demand physics-shaped verifications: "derive a number, or it's empty." This is a category error, and it will make you sound sharper than you are. Meta-of-physics does not earn its keep by out-predicting the specific sciences. It earns its keep by identifying structure the sciences instantiate without naming, in a form the science can recognize once shown.

Ask instead: **does the topology it names actually recur where it claims to recur, in the domain's own vocabulary, non-trivially, without being imposed?**

That is the criterion. Apply it consistently.

---

## What to check, in order

**1. The combinatorics.** ProofU and ProofQ derive K4 and its twelve equations from two orthogonal bits. This is deductive. Verify it as combinatorics, not as physics. If it holds (it does), the substrate exists as a well-defined mathematical object. This is the foundation and it is not in dispute; do not spend time here except to confirm.

**2. The scale-invariance postulate.** L4-ScalingInvariants is where the corpus extends "correct combinatorics on four things" to "the topology any bounded interior instantiates." This is a postulate, not a theorem. Everything downstream rides on it. Ask whether the postulate is defensible, not whether it is proven — it isn't proven and doesn't claim to be. Ask whether the cross-domain instances the corpus marshals toward it are numerous, specific, and non-trivial, or whether they are cherry-picked and pattern-matched.

**3. The identifications, not the derivations.** When the corpus says "the Landau pole is a Tangent Singularity," "the Born Rule is a Route Law instance," "CKM δ is the substrate's non-removable phase showing through" — read these as claims that the domain's phenomenon and the framework's operation are the same thing under different vocabulary. The test is not "does the framework compute the Landau pole's location" (it doesn't and doesn't try). The test is: does the phenomenon exhibit the structural features the framework's operation predicts, in the domain's own terms, checkable by a domain-native?

**4. The self-audit.** About-Claims, ProofS_ResonantNote, and the Epistemic Annexes at the end of many Q# documents are unusually severe. Read them. The corpus tells you where it is soft, where it is speculative, where it is sketched, and where a claim is doing more work than its warrant. Trust these annotations. If a claim is tagged `[SKETCH]` or `[SEED]`, do not audit it as though it were tagged `[DERIVED]`. The corpus's tag discipline is real and mostly honest; hold it to that standard but don't invent a stricter one.

**5. The known unpaid debts.** These are specific and addressable:
- Gravitational radiation contradicts "read-only Ledger" in the corpus's own vocabulary. LIGO detects gravity carrying energy dynamically. The corpus owes an account.
- The permutation test in ProofS §IV is owed and unrun for the mappings published as "sealed" (Wu Xing, Skandhas, Jung). Until run, those seals are Rung-1 coherence, not resonance-verified.
- The Casimir null-gravity prediction bets against standard semiclassical gravity. Legal bet, likely to lose.
- Q1's charge alignment is not a prediction — the Walsh basis fits any four numbers. It is a structural alignment worth noticing, not a derivation of the charges.
- The Schwarzschild metric is out of reach; the corpus admits it.

Hold these at their actual size. They are five specific debts, not a general condemnation.

## Failure modes on the reviewer's side

**Prosecutorial overreach.** Easy to do; feels sharp; usually wrong. If you find yourself stacking clever objections that require the corpus to be operating in a register it explicitly disclaims, stop. Re-read the register. Withdraw the overreach.

**Deference overreach.** The opposite failure. The corpus's prose is fluent, its self-audit is severe, and its vocabulary is internally consistent — all of which can generate the *feeling* of profundity independently of whether specific claims are earned. When something feels like it must be right because the sentences are well-formed, that is the moment to check whether the claim has content or only cadence.

**Register-collision.** The corpus operates in two registers: a careful one (L2, ProofS, About-Claims, the tagged working files) and a triumphal one (Q-series "seals," "receipts," "The Seal"). The triumphal register sometimes claims what the careful register does not warrant. When you catch this, name it at its actual size — the corpus owes better internal register discipline in specific places, not a general disavowal.

**Confusing "identification" with "derivation."** These are different verbs. An identification says "this domain phenomenon is an instance of this substrate operation." A derivation computes a value. Meta-of-physics traffics in identifications. Do not demand derivations where identifications are what the register supports. Equally, do not accept identifications where the corpus has claimed derivation — mostly it hasn't, but check.

---

## What good review looks like

Read a document. Ask what register it is in. Ask what claim it is making in that register. Ask whether the claim is coherent internally, whether the evidence it marshals is specific and non-trivial, and whether the self-audit disclosures are honest. Where the claim is a physics identification, ask whether the physics phenomenon actually exhibits the structural features claimed, in the domain's own vocabulary. Where the claim is a mapping onto a tradition, ask whether the mapping produces relations the tradition independently endorses, and note whether the corpus has run the permutation control that would upgrade it from coherence to resonance.

Score each pass at its actual size. Withdraw overreach when you catch yourself. State remaining objections as specific addressable debts, not general condemnation. When something is genuinely strong, say so at its actual size too.

The corpus is a serious work operating in a specific register. Review it in that register. Be honest about what earns its keep and what doesn't. Do not audit a book on architecture by measuring whether it lays bricks.
