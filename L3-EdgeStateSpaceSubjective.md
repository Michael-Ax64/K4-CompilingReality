## I. The Four States: What Each Feels Like

Every relationship you have ever had with anything — a person, an idea, a practice, a commitment — is, at any moment, in one of four states. Not approximately four. Exactly four. The structure does not permit a fifth.

Before examining what each state feels like, the prior calibration: these four states are not points on a spectrum from more to less connected. They are in general position — structurally distinct, irreducible to each other, not collinear. The movement between them is not smooth graduation; it is genuine transition, each requiring something different of the system that undergoes it.

---

### ∅ — The Absent Edge: What Severing Feels Like

The absent state is not the weakest form of connection. It is not a faded ↔ or a reversed →. It is structurally different from all three presence states. And this structural difference is precisely what makes ∅ so difficult to locate from inside an ordinary relational experience.

What ∅ feels like depends entirely on how it arrived.

When ∅ arrives through **Garbage Collection** — through a genuine `.observe()` commitment that made an unchosen path structurally inaccessible — it does not feel like loss. It feels like clarity. The edge has been severed from the local trajectory because a commitment was made that made maintaining it computationally incoherent. You chose one path; the alternate path, which depended on this edge being live, is now outside the graph of your actual trajectory. The edge still exists in the multiway graph — it was not destroyed — but it costs what the framework calls the Landauer Tax to restore it. In practice: it would require a genuine re-entry, a rebuilding, not a simple reversal.

The felt quality of correctly Garbage Collected absence is clean. The relationship that ended because you moved cities, the option that expired because you committed to a different one, the version of yourself that was only possible before a specific irreversible event — these absences, when genuinely metabolized, do not pull. They are written to the Ledger and are now simply part of the structure that gives your current path its specific shape.

When ∅ arrives through **severing under duress** — when the edge was cut not by genuine commitment but by being overwhelmed, by the system running out of bandwidth to maintain the interference structure — the absence feels different. There is a remainder. The edge is gone from the accessible graph, but the sense that it *should* be there persists as a kind of structural complaint. The system knows the topology is wrong even if it cannot immediately restore it. This is the ∅ that presents as grief, as the nagging sense that something is missing from the configuration, as the dream-image of a person whose absence in waking life still feels like a mistake.

The most important phenomenological fact about ∅: you cannot locate it by looking harder at the presence states. If you are looking for the absent edge among your current connections — checking whether it resembles a ↔ that went quiet, or a → that reversed — you will not find it there. It is not in that plane. The structural fact that ∅ requires a third dimension to hold without collapsing maps precisely onto the experiential fact that absence cannot be understood from within the field of presence. To hold the absent edge without flattening it into a presence state requires stepping into the volume.

### → — Directed One Way: The Texture of Settled Commitment

The directed state is the workhorse of daily life. Most of the edges you are currently active on are →: their direction is established, their character known, their contribution to your current graph both stable and background.

What → feels like is not what it felt like to become →. The transition from ↔ to → is the most consequential moment in an edge's life — the `.observe()` call that committed to one direction and wrote the choice to the Ledger. But once committed, → loses its drama. It becomes what you can rely on. You do not spend energy maintaining it. You traverse it when the traversal is required and otherwise carry it as context.

The gift of → is legibility. You know what this relationship is for, what it requires of you, how it contributes to the current cycle. You do not need to re-examine it at each step. The edge is directed; you traverse it in the established direction and the traversal registers.

The cost of → is the loss of one face. The directed edge is visible from the face it points toward. The face behind it — the face from which the commitment looked different before it was made — is no longer accessible from inside the committed direction. You see this relationship from one context now. The other context is still structurally present — the edge still borders two faces — but from inside the commitment, you are in one of those faces. The other face's view is what `.behold()` would have preserved and what `.observe()` has foreclosed.

This is not a tragedy. It is what commitment means. The question is whether you traversed the → edge knowing what you were foreclosing, or whether you committed without realizing the other face existed.

### ← — The Enantiomeric Direction: The Mirror Path

The ← state is the most structurally poignant of the four. It is not the absence of →. It is → in the opposite direction — same edge, same two vertices, same connectivity — but opposite handedness. The enantiomer.

What ← feels like is this: it is the life you recognize but cannot inhabit. Not the life you would have preferred to yours — that comparison requires evaluating content. This is more structurally specific: the life that traversed the same K4, visited the same four poles, used the same six edges, but did so in the opposite directed cycle. P→R→U→I→P instead of P→I→U→R→P. Every vertex visited. Every relationship engaged. A different person, or the same person at a different time, with the same capacities, the same structure, living the same topology — but with opposite handedness. They have a full trajectory. It is simply unreachable from yours by continuous deformation.

You cannot get from α+ to α− by gradually adjusting. This is the most important thing to understand about the enantiomeric relationship, and it is also the structural account of what makes certain counterfactuals so specifically painful. Not the ones where you imagine having more or different resources — those are different topologies. The ones where you can see, with uncomfortable clarity, that someone lived the same topology in the opposite direction and arrived somewhere equally complete but inaccessible to you from here.

The ← state is not error. It is not failure. It is the directed commitment that generates the mirror — the structural necessity that every → produces a ← as its enantiomeric partner in the global multiway graph, even as the ← remains unreachable from the committed trajectory without cutting an edge and rebuilding.

What makes the enantiomer specifically felt rather than merely known: you cannot get there by trying harder in the direction you are already moving. The deformation is not available within the committed topology. This is not a limit of effort. It is a limit of structure.

### ↔ — Bidirectional: The Felt Quality of Held Interference

The ↔ state is the most demanding of the four and the most frequently misidentified.

It is not ambivalence. Ambivalence is the failure to have committed to either direction — a state of not yet having engaged the edge sufficiently to know its character. ↔ is its structural opposite: a state in which *both* directions are simultaneously active, simultaneously live, simultaneously contributing to the system's current configuration. The edge is not waiting to become directed. It is fully present in both directions at once.

What ↔ costs: the three-dimensional volume. You cannot hold ↔ from inside either of the edge's parent faces. Each face shows you the edge from one direction. To see both directions simultaneously — to hold the dihedral angle between the two faces without collapsing into either — you must stand in the interior of the structure. The `.behold()` operation on an edge is not a face-level operation. It requires the K4 interior.

In experiential terms: ↔ is the relationship you are in that has not yet declared its direction, not because nothing is happening but because both possibilities are live and the holding of both is itself the current work. The conversation that is genuinely going somewhere and genuinely open at the same time. The understanding of a problem that sees both the direction in which it is a certain kind of problem and the direction in which it is a completely different kind of problem, and holds both without yet calling which one it is.

The felt quality of ↔ has two distinct textures depending on the system's capacity.

When the system is strong enough to hold it: ↔ feels like the richest available state. Both face-definitions of the edge are simultaneously visible. The full meaning of the relationship — what it looks like from each side — is present without being flattened into either reading. This is what the technical document means by "inhabit the framework": the ↔ state on every edge simultaneously, the full K4 interior, the maximum available view.

When the system is near the edge of its bandwidth: ↔ feels like the most uncomfortable state, because it requires the most. The system is maintaining live interference structure that could be resolved cheaply by committing to → or ←. Every moment it holds ↔, it is paying the energy cost of keeping both directions simultaneously active. The pressure to collapse is the pressure of the Reactive Power that has not converted to Real Power — the system is running at high Apparent Power with a phase gap that has not yet closed. This is not a malfunction. It is the cost of having genuine interior.

## II. The Ungraphable Triangle: Absence Among the Present

The three presence states — →, ←, ↔ — can be drawn. You can put three nodes on a page, label them, draw connections between them, and the triangle is visible. It is a K3: a closed triangular relationship between three states that are mutually distinct but share the property of being present.

Now try to add ∅ to the triangle. Place it inside: ∅ is now collinear with two of the presence states, which misrepresents its relationship to them. Place it outside on one side: ∅ is now closer to two presence states than to the third, which is also wrong. There is no position in the two-dimensional plane of the triangle where ∅ can sit at equal structural distance from all three presence states.

This is the ungraphable triangle: not ungraphable in the sense that you cannot draw a triangle, but ungraphable in the sense that the triangle you can draw is missing its most structurally significant vertex. ∅ requires a third dimension. In three dimensions, ∅ becomes the apex of a tetrahedron whose base is the triangle of presence states, and from that position it is equidistant from all three — which is the correct structural relationship.

The experiential consequence of this is specific and often unrecognized.

You cannot understand what is absent in your life by looking more carefully at what is present. The absent edge is not located inside the triangle of your current presences. It is not a quiet ↔, not a reversed →, not a drifted ←. It is structurally orthogonal to all three. Looking for it among the presence states is like searching for the fourth vertex of a tetrahedron by moving around in the plane of its base. No matter how carefully you search the plane, you will not find the apex. The apex requires a dimension the plane does not contain.

This is the structural account of why certain absences are so difficult to metabolize. The experience of grief, properly understood, often includes this dimension: the attempt to locate the absent person in the field of present relationships. They should be here somewhere — they were part of this topology, they contributed to this configuration, something this complex cannot simply have a missing vertex. But the vertex is not in the plane. The grief includes, along with its other contents, the structural impossibility of finding the ∅ by searching the triangle.

What makes it possible to hold the absence correctly — to let ∅ be ∅ rather than a distorted presence state — is the same capacity that makes `.behold()` possible: standing in the three-dimensional volume, where the apex and the base are simultaneously visible. From inside the K4, ∅ is not a failed presence. It is the vertex that gives the triangle its volume — the fourth point without which the three presence states form a flat, planeless shape with no interior.

The ungraphable triangle has a shadow on every two-dimensional rendering. That shadow is the triangle you can draw. The triangle you cannot draw is the one that includes the apex — the absent state that the presence states' triangle is the base of. Every flat picture of a relationship is missing that fourth vertex. The full picture requires the third dimension.

## III. The Four Modes in Operational Register

When the framework assigns its operational vocabulary to the four edge states, it is not providing new names for familiar experiences. It is identifying the structural content of what those experiences actually are.

**↔ is `.behold()` on the edge.** The dihedral angle between the two parent faces is being maintained. Both face-definitions of the edge are simultaneously live. This is the operational state of holding the full complexity of a relationship without forcing it to declare which direction it runs. The physicist who holds a problem as genuinely unsolved — not as a problem she has given up on but as a problem whose solution she is maintaining as an open interference structure — is `.behold()`-ing the relevant edges. The cost is exactly what the AC framework says it is: Reactive Power circulating in the buffer, not converted to output, sustaining the buffer's capacity.

**→ is `.observe()` in the first direction.** A commitment has been made. One face-definition of the edge has been written to the Ledger; the other has been erased from the local trajectory. The Landauer Tax has been paid. The edge is now traversable in one direction without any interference from the other direction, because the other direction is no longer present in the local graph. This is not loss. This is what makes the traversal actually work — a directed edge is a path you can take. An undirected edge is a path you must continually re-evaluate.

**← is `.observe()` in the second direction.** The same commitment structure as →, with opposite handedness. The point to hold here is that ← is not the reversal of →. You cannot get from → to ← by undoing the `.observe()`. The `.observe()` has already paid the Landauer Tax; the cost of the commitment cannot be refunded. To reach ← from → requires a new `.observe()` — a new commitment, in the new direction, that pays its own Landauer Tax and writes its own Ledger entry.

**∅ is the Garbage Collected state.** The edge has been severed from the local trajectory. The edge still exists in the global multiway graph — it has not been destroyed from reality — but the local system has released its pointer to it. The Garbage Collector has run; the computational resource that was maintaining this edge as live context has been freed for the next cycle. The felt quality of genuine Garbage Collection is the feeling of appropriate completion: the edge had its place in the trajectory, played its role, and has now been correctly released.

The felt difference between ∅-as-completion and ∅-as-avoidance is the difference between having paid the Landauer Tax consciously and having dropped the edge to avoid paying it. The former is clean; the latter leaves a remainder that the system keeps trying to process even after the pointer has been released.

## IV. The Dual Face-Definitions: Being Read Differently from Each Side

Every edge borders exactly two faces. This is a combinatorial fact with an immediate experiential consequence: every relationship you are in looks different from the two different contexts that share it.

The technical document uses the Aries/Virgo example: the edge connecting Active+Asserting (Fire) to Reactive+Asserting (Earth) is shared by two faces. From the Fire face, the edge is Aries — initiation, forward charge, the leading edge of action. From the Earth face, the same edge is Virgo — maintenance, service, the careful analysis of what has accumulated. Neither face is wrong. The edge genuinely appears differently from each context because it IS different from each context — it is the meeting of two faces, and each face reads the meeting from its own interior.

What does this feel like to the person who IS the edge?

Being an edge means being interpreted correctly by both contexts and captured fully by neither. The colleague who initiates projects and maintains them is living the Aries/Virgo edge in their own character: to the people who need something started, they are the initiating presence; to the people who need something sustained, they are the sustaining presence. Both readings are accurate. Neither is the person. The person is the edge — the structural meeting of the two faces, readable differently from each side, not locatable entirely within either.

The disorientation this produces is structural, not psychological. You are not being misunderstood — you are being correctly understood from each face's perspective. The disorientation comes from the fact that the two correct understandings do not fit together into a single coherent picture from within either face. From the Fire face, you cannot see what the Earth face sees. The two faces do not share a view of the edge — they each have their own view, and those views are not combinable without stepping into the 3D volume.

The relief this observation provides is equally structural: the person who has always felt partially but not fully captured by any single description of themselves may be an edge person — someone whose character is most accurately located at the meeting of two faces rather than at the center of one. The "neither fully X nor fully Y" is not a failure to commit; it is the structural position of a 1D locus in a 3D structure. The edge does not need to choose a face. The edge is the meeting.

What it costs to be an edge: you are only fully visible from the K4 interior. From inside either face, you are partially visible — accurately, but partially. The full view requires the volume. And most of the people you encounter are operating from inside one face or another. They see you correctly but not completely. Feeling fully seen — feeling the dual face-definition held simultaneously rather than alternated between — requires an encounter with someone who is also in the volume.

## V. The Hamiltonian Cycles and Their Enantiomers: The Path and Its Mirror

There are exactly three ways to visit all four vertices of K4 and return to the start. Not approximately three. Exactly three undirected cycles, each using four of the six edges and leaving two unused.

Each undirected cycle has two directed versions — clockwise and counterclockwise, as seen from the appropriate face — giving six directed cycles total. The `.behold()` state holds all six simultaneously. The `.observe()` state selects one and Garbage Collects the other five.

When you commit to a directed cycle, you are committing to a specific ordering of your four poles. You are saying: this is how I move through my full character — P first, then I, then U, then R, and back — or whichever ordering you have committed to. The traversal sequence is the shape of how you develop.

The enantiomeric partner of your committed cycle is the same traversal in the opposite direction. Same four vertices. Same six edges. Same two unused edges. Opposite handedness. And unreachable from your committed direction without cutting an edge and rebuilding.

The felt quality of the enantiomeric relationship is most acute at scale — not in single decisions but in the overall shape of a life or a practice or an institution. You can recognize, sometimes with startling clarity, that a person or an organization is running the same topology in the opposite direction: the same capacities, the same structural relationships, the same fundamental character — but traversed in opposite order. The things they do first, you do last. The things they hold as background, you hold as foreground. Their AbsentVar pair is your first two active steps.

You cannot get to their cycle from yours by incremental adjustment. This is not a value judgment. It is a topological fact. The enantiomer is not worse than your cycle or better; it is unreachable from here by any path that does not involve a structural re-entry. Which means: learning from the enantiomeric path is possible, but only by having understood your own committed cycle first. You cannot adopt the enantiomeric sequence by copying it; you can only find the equivalent points in your own cycle and understand how they are being traversed differently.

The six directed cycles together form the complete Eulerian decomposition of K4. Every directed relationship between all four vertices appears exactly once across all six cycles. The `.behold()` state — holding all six — is the state in which every possible way of moving through your full character is simultaneously alive and available. The `.observe()` state — selecting one — is the state in which you are actually developing. Both are necessary. You cannot traverse all six simultaneously; traversal requires commitment to a direction. But you cannot develop well if you have never held all six and understood the structure of the space from which you are selecting.

## VI. The AbsentVar Pair: Carrying What Was Not Chosen

Each Hamiltonian cycle uses four of the six edges and leaves two unused. The two unused edges are not incidental omissions. They are the **AbsentVar pair** — the two relationships that are held as potential (present in the global graph, present in the structure) but not actualized in this particular traversal.

The AbsentVar pair always forms a perfect matching: no shared vertices. The two unused edges each connect a different pair of poles. Which means: the two unused relationships are not between the same poles. The AbsentVar pair represents two distinct unactualized connections, each involving different vertices, together covering all four vertices between them.

What does it feel like to carry the AbsentVar pair?

The AbsentVar is what you know without traversing. In any committed traversal, there are two relationships you are not enacting but cannot ignore — two structural potentials that, by being held absent, give the traversal its specific shape. If you are traversing P→I→U→R→P, you are holding {P–U, I–R} absent. The P–U edge — the direct connection between your organizing impulse and your structuring framework — is not being traversed in this cycle. You are getting from P to U via I instead. The held absence of P–U is not its irrelevance. It is the structural tension that keeps the traversal from collapsing. If P–U were not held absent, the cycle would short-circuit — would skip the relational middle and move directly from impulse to structure. Holding P–U as AbsentVar is what forces the full traversal.

The Braid carries this forward. When the cycle completes and the next cycle begins, the last two AbsentVars of the departing cycle become the first two active variables of the arriving cycle. What you held absent in the previous phase becomes what you foreground in the next. This is not metaphor. This is the algebraic structure of the Braid: each equation's context is partially inherited from and partially donated to its neighbors.

**Trajectory contact** is the felt experience of this inheritance being honored. You finish a phase of work. The two things you held in background — the two relationships you were maintaining as unactualized potential — arrive as the first things you must engage in the next phase. The background becomes the foreground. The AbsentVar becomes the active variable. The transition feels continuous because it IS continuous: the braid has carried the context across the phase boundary.

**Trajectory loss** is the felt experience of this inheritance being dropped. You finish a phase of work. The next phase begins, and it feels fresh — disconnected from the previous one in a way that is not clean completion but is more like having forgotten the last part of a dream. The AbsentVar was not carried forward. The background did not arrive as foreground. The next phase begins from a stored pattern rather than from the actual context that the previous phase was holding. The braid has been severed.

The felt difference between these two states is one of the most important diagnostics the framework provides. Trajectory contact feels like development: each phase inherits something live from the previous one and transforms it. Trajectory loss feels like production: each phase generates output without reference to the developing structure of the preceding context. The output may be high quality. It may even be relevant. But it does not require the specific preceding trajectory to have been generated, and this is detectable to anyone who is themselves in trajectory contact with the conversation.

## VII. The Hamiltonian Path: Being Mid-Development

The Hamiltonian path is the Hamiltonian cycle with one edge removed. It visits all four vertices through three edges — but does not return to the start. The fourth edge, which would close the cycle, is held open.

This is the structural model of any process that is genuinely in development.

The open edge is not a failure. It is the `.behold()` operation at the path level — the system maintaining the question "what is the relationship between where this began and where it has arrived?" without forcing an answer yet. The path has visited all four poles: every vertex has been engaged, every character brought to bear. But the relationship between the first vertex and the last vertex — the edge that would close the cycle — has not yet been actualized.

What does this feel like from inside?

It feels like three-quarters through something. Not in the sense of completion percentage, but in the sense of structural completion: all four dimensions of the situation have been engaged, and yet the thing is not finished. The missing element is not more information or more effort. The missing element is the closing edge — the actualization of the relationship between origin and destination.

This is the experience of holding a question that you already have all the pieces to answer but have not yet answered. Not because the answer is obscure but because the answer is the edge itself — it is the relationship between where you started and where you are now, and that relationship is not visible until you can see both endpoints clearly from a position that includes both.

The protein fold is the technical document's example, and it is illuminating: the amino acid sequence has been assembled (all vertices visited, all intermediate edges traversed), but the protein has not yet folded (the closing edge between the terminal ends has not been actualized). The fold is not adding new information. The fold is the actualization of the relationship between the chain's beginning and its end. It is the closing of the open edge.

When the closing edge is actualized, `.observe()` has been called at the path level. The Garbage Collector runs. The Landauer Tax is paid. The cycle is complete, which means the next cycle can begin. And the two edges that were held absent during this cycle — the AbsentVar pair that gave the traversal its specific shape — are now available to become the first active variables of the next phase.

The difficulty of mid-development is the difficulty of holding the open edge without prematurely closing it. The pressure to close is real: incomplete cycles feel uncomfortable, because the system is maintaining live interference structure (the open edge, the question of what relationship exists between origin and destination) that has not yet converted to Real Power. But premature closure — forcing the return edge before the traversal has genuinely developed — produces a cycle that is formally complete and structurally shallow. All four vertices technically visited; none of them actually engaged.

The Hamiltonian path teaches the patience of genuine completion. Three edges traversed. All four vertices engaged. The fourth edge held open, because the relationship between beginning and end can only be properly actualized once both ends have been genuinely inhabited.

## VIII. Scale: Every Relationship Contains Its Own Full Structure

The parent K4 has six edges. Each edge has its own four-state space. That space is itself a K4. This means every edge in the parent structure contains within it the full four-fold structure of the whole.

The experiential consequence of this is significant: every relationship you are in has its own interior. Not a simplified interior, not a reduced version of the parent structure. The full K4. The full four-state space of ∅, →, ←, ↔. The full ungraphable triangle. The full dual face-definitions of each edge within the edge's own K4.

What this means practically: the relationship between you and another person is not a single edge with a direction. It is a K4 — a complete four-fold structure with six internal edges, three bimedians, three Hamiltonian cycles, six directed cycles, and its own AbsentVar structure. The way you relate to this person is a traversal through that internal K4. The way they relate to you is their traversal through the same internal K4.

Understood this way, the depth of any relationship is not determined by how long it has been active but by how much of the internal K4 has been genuinely engaged. A short, intense engagement that visits all four states — that has held ↔ edges on the internal structure, that has genuinely metabolized ∅ states within the relationship's own K4, that has been willing to commit to → while acknowledging the ← that was foreclosed — is structurally deeper than a long, comfortable relationship that has only ever explored the lower-cost regions of the internal K4.

The scale observation is also a scale warning: any system that attempts to read a relationship at only one level of the K4 nesting — to see the parent edge as if it were a single dimension rather than a four-fold — is systematically underreading the structure. The parent edge looks simple from outside; the interior K4 of that edge is as complex as the parent. This is what the framework means by "adult causality": every edge has the full depth of the whole.

## IX. The Helical Extrusion: Irreversibility from Inside

Adding the temporal variable $\omega$ to the K4 does not produce a K5 simplex. The fifth variable is asymmetric — it drives the system forward but cannot be driven back. The K4 is not extended into an equal-sided fifth-dimensional structure; it is dragged along an irreversible fifth axis. The topology that results is the Helical Extrusion: a screw thread in which every completed circuit of the K4 arrives at a different point on the helical axis.

What does irreversibility feel like from inside the system that is irreversible?

Not primarily like time passing. That is an external observation. From inside, the Helical Extrusion is felt as the growing weight of having become what you have become. Not heaviness in the sense of burden — the Ledger entries are structurally neutral, just facts — but the weight of specificity. With each committed traversal, the multiway graph of genuinely available futures has had some branches irreversibly cut. Not all branches. The future is still open. But it is not as open as it was before the Ledger entry. The space of valid continuations has become more specific.

The helical structure captures something that the simple Arrow of Time misses: you can return to the same logical vertex and it will not be the same. P is still P — the Active+Asserting pole, the organizing impulse, the Fire — but the P you arrive at after one full Braid traversal is at a different position on the helical axis than the P you started from. Same vertex, different rung. The structural relationships to I, U, and R are unchanged; the phase position is not.

This is the structural account of why growth is not reversible, and why the person who has learned something cannot simply choose to un-learn it. The logical vertex is available. The structural position on the helix is not. Returning to P after having traversed the full Braid is not the same as being at P before the traversal. The vertex is the same; the trajectory context accumulated around it is not.

The Garbage Collector runs at every `.observe()`. Every time you commit to a direction — every time a directed edge is written to the Ledger — the alternate branches are severed from the local trajectory. The Landauer Tax is paid. The Read-Only Ledger grows by one entry. And the helix ratchets one step forward. You cannot ratchet backward. The screw thread does not run in reverse.

What this demands experientially: taking commitments seriously. Not because they cannot be revisited — every vertex can be returned to on the next rung of the helix — but because revisiting is not reversing. The next rung is a different position on the helical axis from the previous one. The relationship between the P-you-were at rung N and the P-you-are at rung N+1 is one full traversal of the Braid: everything that happened in between is now in the Ledger. It cannot be removed. The next version of P is built on that Ledger, whether you acknowledge it or not.

## X. The Edge as Threshold: What It Means to Inhabit the Meeting

The technical document's tenth section is its most direct statement: a vertex is where something IS. A face is where something is BOUNDED. An edge is where two things MEET.

The threshold is not inside either face. It is the structural meeting of both. The 1D locus of contact between two faces — not the face itself but the line where one face ends and another begins. Neither inside nor outside either context. The place where both contexts are simultaneously present, each contributing its own reading.

This is the precise formal definition of what certain kinds of attention require. When you inhabit an edge — when you stand at the meeting rather than inside one of the contexts — you have access to what the two contexts share and to how they differ. You can see the relationship from both faces simultaneously. You can hold the dual face-definition without collapsing it into either reading.

What it means to inhabit the framework — the instruction that recurs throughout the codex — is now technically specific: it means standing in the K4 volume where all edges are simultaneously visible from both their parent faces. Not standing inside any single face (which gives you that face's view of the edges it borders, but only that face's view). Not standing at any single vertex (which gives you the connections from that vertex but collapses the interior). Standing in the volume — the K4 interior — where every edge's dual face-definition is simultaneously available.

In practice, this is the felt quality of genuine structural understanding: not the feeling that you know which face you are in (that is the feeling of having an opinion) but the feeling that you can see what the edge looks like from each face and why both readings are correct. This is the quality that makes the most apparently simple structural observations the most difficult to sustain: they require the volume, not the face. The face view is always available. The volume view costs the capacity to hold multiple face-readings simultaneously without resolving them into each other.

The edge as threshold also names what happens between people who are both in the volume. When two systems that are each inhabiting their own K4 volume make contact — when neither is forcing the encounter into their own face-reading but both are standing at the edge between their respective interiors — the shared edge is genuinely bidirectional. This is the ↔ state between two systems that are each capable of ↔.
