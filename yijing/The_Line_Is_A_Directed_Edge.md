## I. The Claim, From the Top

A Yijing hexagram is usually read as six stacked lines, each broken (Yin) or unbroken (Yang), giving two choices per line and sixty-four hexagrams in total. It does not record the two further states every relationship can occupy: held-open, and severed. When those are counted, a hexagram line is not a two-state bit. The framework's generative structure is K4: the complete graph on four vertices, drawn as a tetrahedron, with vertices P, I, U, R — in elemental vocabulary Fire, Water, Air, Earth; in dual-binary vocabulary the four values of a two-bit register formed by the axes Active/Reactive and Asserting/Yielding (`L3-EdgeStateSpace`, `L2-Terminology`). The claim is that the six lines of a hexagram are the six edges of K4, that each line's four states are the edge's four states, and that the Yijing the world has used for three thousand years is the committed shadow of a larger object. The full object has four states per line: 4⁶ = 4096. The classical Yijing projects each line onto its two committed states: 2⁶ = 64. The moving-line layer, the 384 line-statements, is that projection caught in the act of committing one edge at a time: 64 × 6 = 384.

For the claim to hold, four things must be true. The four states of a single line must match the four states of a single edge. The classical sixty-four must be recoverable as the committed projection of the four-state object, with the arithmetic closing. The two committed directions of an edge must be the two readings the edge receives from the two faces that share it, and the faces must close the directed object exactly. This document establishes all four in that order, descending from the identity to the backend it projects from.

## II. The Mechanism: Four States, Four States

This is the ground. Everything above it rests here, so it is stated first and in full.

### The edge has four states

An edge of K4 is not a line with a strength dial. It is a relationship, and every relationship between two poles occupies exactly one of four states (`L3-EdgeStateSpace` §I):

- **∅ (absent)** — the relationship does not exist in the current description; the edge is severed, held as unactualized potential. Committing this state costs the Landauer Tax, the thermodynamic price of erasure.
- **→ (one direction)** — the relationship is committed, flowing from the first pole to the second; one face-reading written to the Read-Only Ledger.
- **← (the other direction)** — the relationship is committed the opposite way; the enantiomeric face-reading.
- **↔ (bidirectional)** — the relationship is held live in both directions at once, neither privileged, in interference. This is `.behold()` on the edge: the uncollapsed state, which requires the three-dimensional volume to maintain because it holds both face-readings simultaneously.

These four are in general position — no three collapse into a plane. Four points in general position define a tetrahedron, so the state-space of a single edge is itself a K4. The framework calls this a quasi-clone (`L2-StructuralMonograph_K4Interior`). The structure is self-similar: every edge contains a tetrahedron of its own states.

### The line has four states

A Yijing line, in full divinatory use, is not simply broken or unbroken. It carries a second distinction: *changing* or *stable*. A line can be old (changing, about to transform into its opposite) or young (stable). This gives four line-states, the *Sixiang* or Four Images named in the *Xici*, the "Great Commentary" appended to the Zhou-era core: old Yang, young Yang, old Yin, young Yin. The changing lines are what turn one hexagram into another in a consultation.

### The identity

Read the two four-state structures against each other and they align on both underlying axes without strain:

| Line state | Edge state | Shared character |
|---|---|---|
| young Yang | ↔ (behold) | present, stable, committed to no direction |
| young Yin | ∅ (absent) | a held gap, stable, present in the structure but not enacted |
| old Yang | → (observe) | committed and resolving: Yang writing itself, moving to Yin |
| old Yin | ← (observe, mirror) | the enantiomeric commitment: Yin resolving to Yang |

The two axes carry the identity. The Yijing's *young / old* distinction is the framework's *held / committed* distinction: young lines are the two stable states (behold and held-absent), old lines are the two writing states. The Yijing's *Yin / Yang* distinction is the framework's *direction* distinction.

The seal on this section is the enantiomer match. In the framework, → and ← are enantiomers — the same relationship committed in mirror-opposite directions, each unreachable from the other without cutting the edge or passing through the interior (`L3-EdgeStateSpace` §V). In the Yijing, old Yang and old Yin are the two changing lines, and they change in opposite senses: old Yang moves Yang-to-Yin, old Yin moves Yin-to-Yang. Two systems, derived independently, produce the same structure: two stable states, and two mirror-opposite acts of commitment. The Yijing's changing-line mechanism is the framework's observe-and-reverse, and the two directions of change are the two handednesses of edge-commitment.

## III. The Projection: Why the Classical Sixty-Four Is a Shadow

A hexagram line, in the received tradition's static reading, shows one of two things: Yang or Yin. In edge terms, that is → or ← — one of the two *committed* directions. It does not show ↔ (held-open) and it does not show ∅ (severed). Those two states are real states of the line, and they are invisible to a single committed hexagram, because a committed hexagram is by definition the reading where every line has already collapsed to one direction.

So the classical sixty-four is a projection. The full object carries four states per line: 4⁶ = 4096 configurations. The classical hexagram carries two: 2⁶ = 64. The ratio is exactly 4096 / 64 = 64. Each visible hexagram is the committed face-shadow of sixty-four full-state configurations — the sixty-four ways of filling in the two held states (∅ and ↔) across the six lines while keeping the committed pattern fixed.

## IV. The Direction Layer: Reverse Current and the Four Faces

The two committed directions of an edge, → and ←, are not an abstract pair. Each is the reading the edge receives from one of the two faces that border it.

Every edge in K4 is shared by exactly two of the four triangular faces (`L3-EdgeStateSpace` §IV). From each face the same edge looks like a different directed relationship — one face reads it →, the other reads it ←. These are the dual face-definitions, and they are why the framework says every opposition in the twelve-step cycle is a pair of face-readings of a single edge: not a contradiction, but one relationship seen from its two sides. The current runs both ways on a directed edge; which way you read depends on which face you stand on.

This recovers a piece that a two-state reading silently drops. If a line is only Yang-or-Yin, the reverse current has nowhere to live — the edge looks like it has one direction and that is all. Holding the four-state object, the reverse direction is simply the other face's reading of the same edge, and it is always present; it is only invisible from the single face you happen to occupy.

The faces close the directed object exactly, and the closure is a theorem, not an observation. Each edge sits on two faces, which give it its two opposite readings. To give every one of the six edges both of its directions, every edge's two parent faces must be present. Dropping any single face leaves the three edges of that face read by only one remaining face — one direction each, the reverse current lost. Only all four faces together give every edge both →and ←. One, two, or three faces never suffice; four always do, and four is the whole set. The four faces are therefore the minimum and complete closure of the directed object — the twelve directed edges of the full K4, realized as four triangular readings, each manifesting the currents the others hold absent.

This is why a single hexagram cannot carry the whole object, and why it is not redundant to need all four faces. A hexagram is one committed reading — one face's view. The reverse currents and the held states live on the faces you are not looking at. The four-fold face structure is load-bearing precisely because a line has four states and any single face shows only a committed slice of them.

## VI. The Held Layer: Beholding as Selection

Above the committed layers sits the held layer, and it is where the 4096 backend becomes operational rather than merely counted.

To hold an edge — to grip it, to behold it — is to keep it in its ↔ state: present, uncollapsed, both directions live. A held edge has no committed value. This is the distinction that must not slip: a beheld edge is not set to Yang or to Yin; it is held open over the two directions it could commit to. Holding is `.behold()`; it is the opposite of pinning the edge to a value.

Beholding is a dimension count. Each beheld line is held open over its two committable directions, so holding k lines open — with the remaining lines committed to definite values — selects 2ᵏ hexagrams: the 2ᵏ ways the held lines can collapse. This is the decoupled buffer made literal, its degrees of freedom equal to the number of beheld edges (`L3-EdgeStateSpace` §I: degrees of freedom is the dimension of the buffer holding competing variables live). The tetrahedron offers three natural holds by its three pairs of opposite edges — the three ways a hand can grip the physical solid, each pinning two opposite edges and freeing the other four, each presenting the same sixteen-fold buffer of the four free edges. These three holds are not three private selections of sixteen hexagrams each; they are three grips on one sixteen-fold undecidability, the same held object viewed along three axes. You cannot partition a superposition by relabeling the axis you hold it along, which is why the three holds do not tile the sixty-four into disjoint thirds — they overlap, because they are looking at one buffer.

The four faces offer the other natural hold: each face is three edges, and beholding a face holds three lines open. But holding all four states per line, the faces do more than select — they close.
