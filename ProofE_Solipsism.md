## I. The Mathematical Setup

The complex plane consists of numbers of the form a + bi, where a is the real component, b is the imaginary component, and i = √(-1).

The real axis (ℝ) has a crucial algebraic property: it is **closed under multiplication**. Any two real numbers multiplied together produce a real number. 3 × 4 = 12 ∈ ℝ. √2 × π ∈ ℝ. The real axis can sustain itself: real-times-real stays real.

The imaginary axis (iℝ) does not have this property. Two purely imaginary numbers multiplied together do not stay imaginary:

i × i = i² = -1 ∈ ℝ

The product of two purely imaginary numbers exits the imaginary axis and lands on the real axis. The imaginary axis is **not closed under multiplication**. It cannot sustain itself independently of the real axis: imaginary-times-imaginary crosses back to real.

This is not a quirk or an artifact of the notation. It is the algebraic statement of a structural fact: the interior (imaginary component) is constitutively relational with the exterior (real component). It cannot sustain itself independently. The relationship between interior and exterior is not symmetric: ℝ is a subfield of ℂ (a closed algebraic structure within the complex numbers), while iℝ is not. The real axis has a structural privilege not by convention but by algebraic necessity.

## II. The Coordinatization Flip

It is mathematically valid to represent the complex plane in imaginary-first coordinates. Instead of writing a + bi (real primary, imaginary secondary), write bi + a (imaginary primary, real secondary). This is a change of coordinatization — a legitimate choice of how to notate the same mathematical object. The underlying algebraic relationships are unchanged. The complex plane is the same complex plane in either coordinatization.

The flip is useful in specific contexts. In electrical engineering, for instance, reactive power (the imaginary component of complex power) is often the quantity of primary interest, and imaginary-first notation makes the reactive component primary in the representation. This is a legitimate choice for a specific purpose.

The algebraic constraint remains. Under imaginary-first coordinatization, the expressions look different — i²-containing terms now appear as real numbers in the primary position — but the algebraic fact that i² = -1 ∈ ℝ has not changed. The interior still cannot sustain itself independently. Imaginary-times-imaginary still crosses to real, now appearing as the secondary component rather than the primary one. The asymmetry is still there; the coordinatization has changed which component gets written first.

## III. The Solipsistic Error

Solipsism — the philosophical position that only one's own mind is real, and that the external world exists only as a construct of that mind — is the structural error of performing the imaginary-first coordinatization and then forgetting that the flip occurred.

The mind is the imaginary component: the interior, the felt quality of experience, the subjective. The external world is the real component: the exterior, the material, the objective.

In the default coordinatization (real-first): the external world is primary, the mind is how we access and process it. Interior experience is real, but secondary: it requires the external world as the substrate it arises from and remains in contact with.

The solipsist performs the coordinatization flip: the mind becomes primary, and the external world is understood as a construction of the mind. This is not necessarily wrong as a starting point — Descartes' *cogito* is exactly this move, and it is a legitimate methodological choice for specific purposes.

The error: treating the imaginary-first coordinatization as complete and self-sustaining — forgetting that i² = -1 ∈ ℝ. The mind, treated as the primary and self-sufficient reality, will inevitably produce results that cross back to the real component. Every time the purely-interior constructs of the mind interact with each other, they generate something that lands in the exterior world: action, speech, impact, consequence. The imaginary-first universe cannot maintain itself in purely imaginary form. It keeps generating real numbers.

The consistent solipsist is therefore in a structurally unstable position. The coherence of the imaginary-first worldview requires that the "external world" be kept in quotation marks — treated as apparent, constructed, secondary. But the actual behavior of imaginary components under multiplication continuously undermines this: the interior's own operations generate the exterior, over and over, regardless of the theoretical position about whether the exterior is real.

## IV. Individual Solipsism and Its Compensatory Mechanisms

Individual solipsism — the philosophical position held by one person — is relatively rare and relatively self-limiting. The person who genuinely treats the external world as a construction of their own mind quickly runs into practical difficulties: the external world does not consistently behave as their constructions predict, and the gap between prediction and outcome provides continuous feedback that the exterior is not fully controlled by the interior.

More common: **functional solipsism** — not the explicit philosophical position, but the implicit treatment of one's own interior experience as the baseline reality and other people's interior experiences as secondary, derivative, or requiring evidence that they are "real."

The person who treats their own pain as obviously real and requiring no justification, while treating others' pain as requiring evidence and justification before it is acknowledged. The person who treats their own way of perceiving and valuing as obviously correct and requiring no defense, while treating other ways of perceiving and valuing as departures from the correct baseline. The person who experiences their own interior states as directly legible to them, while experiencing other people's interior states as inherently opaque and therefore somewhat less real.

This is not the philosophical position "only my mind is real." It is the functional pattern of treating one's own interior as primary and self-evidently real while treating other interiors as secondary and in need of external validation. The coordinatization flip has been performed and the flip has been forgotten.

The compensatory mechanisms: increasingly elaborate internal models that attempt to account for the exterior world's behavior without granting the exterior genuine independent reality. The models become more sophisticated over time — the functional solipsist is not stupid — but they remain imaginary-first: the exterior is being processed as a construction of the interior rather than engaged with as genuinely other.

The algebraic consequence keeps appearing: the models generate real-component outputs (behavior in the world, impact on other people) that do not match the imaginary-first predictions. Each mismatch requires another internal model update. The models become more complex. The complexity generates more mismatches. The cycle is the Kessler cascade at the individual cognitive level.

## V. Civilizational Solipsism

The more consequential instance: **civilizational solipsism** — the implicit treatment of the human interior (human cognitive categories, human perceptual frameworks, human values and interests) as the baseline reality, with the non-human world as secondary, derivative, or requiring evidence of relevance to human concerns before being granted genuine status.

This is the Idol of the Tribe at maximum scale: the species-level perceptual and cognitive structures of Homo sapiens are being treated as the correct categories for understanding all of reality, not as one set of categories among others.

The coordinatization flip: human consciousness and human experience are placed in the primary position (imaginary-first), and the non-human world — other species, ecosystems, geological and cosmological processes — is treated as the context that surrounds and serves the primary human reality.

The algebraic consequence: i² = -1 ∈ ℝ. The human interior's own operations generate outcomes in the exterior world that cross back to real: ecological disruption, climate change, species loss, geological-scale impacts of industrial activity. The interior (human civilization and its projects) operating on itself generates exterior consequences. The imaginary-first framework cannot fully account for these consequences because it has treated the exterior as secondary to the interior.

The compensatory mechanisms at the civilizational scale: increasingly elaborate intellectual frameworks that attempt to account for the exterior world's behavior without revising the fundamental imaginary-first coordinatization. Environmental economics (putting monetary values on ecosystem services — bringing the exterior into the imaginary-first framework's vocabulary rather than revising the framework). Anthropocentric ethics extended to include other species on the basis of their relevance to human values. Technologies that attempt to manage exterior-world consequences without changing the interior-first orientation that generates them.

The algebraic constraint does not relax under these compensatory mechanisms. The exterior keeps generating real-component outcomes that exceed the imaginary-first framework's capacity to contain or predict them. The compensatory elaborations are correct as far as they go; they do not go far enough because they do not revise the fundamental coordinatization.

## VI. The Correction

The correction to solipsism — individual, functional, or civilizational — is not the elimination of the interior or the privileging of the exterior. It is acknowledging the flip and restoring the algebraic relationship.

ℝ is closed under multiplication; iℝ is not. This means: the exterior (real axis) has a structural privilege — not priority in the sense of being more important, but algebraic necessity in the sense of being what the interior returns to when it operates on itself. The interior is constitutively relational with the exterior. It arose from the exterior, is sustained by the exterior, and continuously generates exterior consequences through its own operations.

The correction is not "the exterior is primary and the interior is secondary." That would be performing the inverse flip — real-first coordinatization — and forgetting the flip in the other direction. The correction is: neither is primary in the sense of being self-sufficient. Both are components of the same complex number. The complex number cannot be adequately described as purely real or purely imaginary. It requires both components, with their specific algebraic relationship, to be what it is.

Interior experience is real. Exterior reality is real. The interior is not a construction of the exterior; the exterior is not a construction of the interior. They are two components of the same system, with a specific algebraic relationship (the exterior is closed under its own operations in a way the interior is not), and neither can be fully understood in isolation from the other.

This is the correction that the framework provides for solipsism: not a philosophical argument for the reality of the external world (which the solipsist can always absorb into their imaginary-first framework), but a structural demonstration of the algebraic relationship between interior and exterior — a demonstration that the interior's own operations generate exterior consequences, that the imaginary axis cannot sustain itself without the real, and that the coordinatization choice does not alter the underlying algebraic facts.
